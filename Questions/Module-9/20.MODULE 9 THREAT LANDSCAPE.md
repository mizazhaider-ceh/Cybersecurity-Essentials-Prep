**Question 1** (ðŸ”´ **CRITICAL - DEFINITION!**)

**Threat Landscape:**

**Definition:**

- **Comprehensive snapshot** of current cybersecurity threat environment
- **Aggregates and synthesizes** threat intelligence data from dozens of sources (feeds, incident reports, surveys, etc.)
- **Presents macro-level trends**, emerging risks, and threat actor behaviors across industries and geographies

**It helps to:**

- **Identify trends:** Dominant attack vectors, top malware families, threat actors
- **Contextualize risk** at organizational and national levels
- **Inform strategic decisions** in security architecture, compliance, and defense prioritization

**True or False:** "Threat landscape provides tactical, incident-specific data."

**Answer:** False (it provides MACRO-level strategic trends, not tactical incident data)

**Question 3** (ðŸ”´ **CRITICAL - THREAT ECOSYSTEM!**)

**Threat Ecosystem Relationships:**

**Threat Landscape:**

- **Macro picture** that sets context and priorities
- Draws from **Threat Intelligence**

**Threat Intelligence:**

- **Specific, actionable data** on IoCs, TTPs, actor behaviors
- Collected from feeds, reports, incident data
- **Feeds into** Threat Landscape

**Threat Modeling:**

- Uses landscape insights to execute **risk identification during design phase**
- Asks: "**Given current threats, what are my design weaknesses?**"
- Modeling outputs **prioritize defenses** needed

**Risk Analysis:**

- Evaluates **likelihood and impact** of threats
- Combines **strategy (landscape)** and **tactical response** (detection, mitigation, patching)
- Prioritizes investments and compliance (e.g., NIS2)

**Which provides macro-level trends: Threat Intelligence or Threat Landscape?**

**Answer:** Threat Landscape

**Question 32** (ðŸ”´ **CRITICAL - VERIS GLOSSARY!**)

**Verizon DBIR Glossary:**

**Asset:**

- The target of the attack

**Actor:**

- **Threat Agent** (who performed attack)

**Action:**

- The **methods** the actor used to cause/contribute to incident
- **7 main categories:** Malware, Hacking, Social Engineering, Misuse, Physical Actions, Errors, Environmental Factors

**Attribute:**

- The **impact** on the asset
- Breach of: **Confidentiality, Integrity/Authenticity, or Availability** (CIA!)

**How many action categories in VERIS?**

**Answer:** 7

***

**Question 33** (ðŸ”´ **CRITICAL - MORE GLOSSARY!**)

**Verizon DBIR Glossary (continued):**

**Vector:**

- **Attack vector** (method of attack)

**Breach:**

- An incident that results in **confirmed disclosure** (not just potential disclosure) of data to **unauthorized party**

**Incident:**

- A security event that compromises **integrity, confidentiality, or availability** of an information asset

**Key Distinction:**

- **Incident** = Any security event compromising CIA
- **Breach** = Incident with **CONFIRMED data disclosure**

**All breaches are incidents, but not all incidents are breaches!**

**True or False:** "All incidents result in data breaches."

**Answer:** False (only incidents with confirmed data disclosure are breaches)

## **PART D: PYRAMID OF PAIN \& MITRE ATT\&CK (Questions 91-120)**

**Question 91** (ðŸ”´ **CRITICAL - PYRAMID DEFINITION!**)

**Pyramid of Pain:**

**Developed:** 2013

**Purpose:**

- Helps cybersecurity professionals **understand and categorize "Indicators of Compromise" (IoCs)**
- Based on **how much they disrupt attackers' operations**

**Indicator of Compromise (IoC):**

- Anything that can be **detected** and that is or can be a **sign** that your system is **compromised** by an attack

**Organization:**

- Six levels from **least to most impactful** on attacker

**Key Insight:**

- **Detecting attacks based on hash values** = Much easier for attacker to circumvent
- **Detecting attacks based on TTPs** = MUCH HARDER for attacker to change

**What year was Pyramid of Pain developed?**

**Answer:** 2013

***

**Question 92** (ðŸ”´ **CRITICAL - PYRAMID LEVELS!**)

**Pyramid of Pain - Six Levels (Bottom to Top):**


| Level | Detection Difficulty | Impact on Attacker |
| :-- | :-- | :-- |
| **1. Hash Values** | Trivial | Easy (for attacker to change) |
| **2. IP Addresses** | Simple | Simple (for attacker to change) |
| **3. Domain Names** | Easy | Easy (for attacker to change) |
| **4. Network/Host Artifacts** | Annoying | Annoying (for attacker to change) |
| **5. Tools** | Challenging | Challenging (for attacker to change) |
| **6. TTPs** | Tough! | **PAINFUL** for attacker to change! |

**Explanation:**

**Bottom (Easy to detect, easy for attacker to evade):**

- **Hash values:** Change one byte â†’ new hash
- **IP addresses:** Get new IP easily
- **Domain names:** Register new domain

**Top (Hard to detect, PAINFUL for attacker to change):**

- **TTPs (Tactics, Techniques, Procedures):** Attacker's entire methodology
- Changing TTPs requires **complete retooling and retraining**!

**What is at the TOP of the Pyramid of Pain?**

**Answer:** TTPs (Tactics, Techniques \& Procedures)

***

**Question 93** (ðŸ”´ **CRITICAL - TTPs DEFINITION!**)

**TTPs = Tactics, Techniques \& Procedures**

**Why are TTPs at top of pyramid?**

- **MOST PAINFUL** for attacker to change
- Represents attacker's **entire approach** and **methodology**
- Changing TTPs requires:
    - Complete **retooling**
    - **Retraining** of personnel (if organized group)
    - **New development** of attack methods
    - Significant **time and resources**

**Example:**

- **Hash value:** Change one byte of malware â†’ new hash (trivial)
- **TTP:** Change from phishing-based attacks to exploiting zero-days â†’ requires completely different skillset, tools, and approach (PAINFUL!)

**Defending based on TTPs = Most effective but also most challenging!**

**What does TTP stand for?**

**Answer:** Tactics, Techniques \& Procedures

**Question 182** (ðŸ”´ **CRITICAL - CLOUD RISKS PART 1!**)

**Cloud Risks:**

**1. Loss of Governance \& Visibility:**

- Customer **gives up some control \& visibility** to cloud provider
- May affect security if **SLAs leave security gaps**

**2. Isolation Failure:**

- Cloud computing uses **shared resources**
- **Failure of separation mechanisms** (storage, memory, routing) between cloud users = risky
- Especially with **smaller cloud providers**

**3. Compliance:**

- Migration to cloud may **risk failing certification** (ISO27001, NIS2)
- If cloud provider **cannot provide compliance evidence**

**Why is isolation failure more risky with smaller providers?**

**Answer:** Less mature separation mechanisms and security practices

**Question 183** (ðŸ”´ **CRITICAL - CLOUD RISKS PART 2!**)

**4. Data Protection:**

- Difficult for customers to **verify cloud provider's security procedures**
- **Data location** concerns (e.g., for GDPR compliance)

**5. Management Interface Compromise:**

- Client's **cloud management interface** = increased risk
- **Accessible via Internet**
- Provides **access to large amounts of resources**

**6. Malicious Insider:**

- Cloud architects have **big responsibility**
- **Malicious insider** can do great damage

**7. Data Flow Risks:**

- Data flows over **Internet**
- Critical that data is **protected from eavesdropping and manipulation**

**Why is management interface compromise so risky?**

**Answer:** Internet-accessible + controls large amounts of resources = attractive target

**Question 184** (ðŸ”´ **CRITICAL - CLOUD RISKS PART 3!**)

**8. Insecure APIs:**

    - **Weak authentication**
    - **Weak encryption**
    - **Weak validation**

**9. Misconfigurations:**

- **Improperly configured cloud settings:**
    - Poorly secured storage buckets
    - Weak IAM (Identity and Access Management) policies

**10. Account Hijacking:**

- Attackers gain access to **users' cloud credentials**
- Can access cloud services
- Potentially cause **major damage**

**Question 262** (ðŸ”´ **CRITICAL - AI TYPES COMPARISON!**)

**AI Type Comparison:**

**Classic AI:**

- **Decision rules:** Created by **human expert**
- **Learning:** **NO** learning from input
- **Example:** Medical diagnosis system where doctor programmed all rules
- **Demo:** symptomate.com

**Machine Learning:**

- **Decision rules:** Created by **computer through learning**
- **Features:** Provided by **humans** (we tell AI what to look for)
- **Learning:** **YES** - learns from training data
- **Example:** Digit classifier learning from thousands of handwritten digit images
- **Training required:** Feed labeled data (input + correct output)

**Deep Learning:**

- **Decision rules:** Created by **computer through learning**
- **Features:** **Extracted by AI itself** (we DON'T tell it what to look for!)
- **Learning:** **YES** - learns from training data
- **Neural Networks:** More complex than ML
- **Example:** Image recognition that discovers "car has 4 wheels" by itself
- **Demo:** TensorFlow

**Example:**

- **Machine Learning:** We tell AI "car has 4 wheels, bike has 2"
- **Deep Learning:** AI **discovers** on its own that cars have 4 wheels!

**What's the key difference between ML and Deep Learning?**

**Answer:** Deep Learning extracts features itself; ML needs humans to provide features
